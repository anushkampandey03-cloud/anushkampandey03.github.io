Circadian Immunology

Your immune system runs on a clock. Tumours have figured out how to hack it.

Complication rates differ measurably depending on when a procedure is scheduled. We've known this empirically for years. We're only now understanding the molecular mechanism — and it points directly to how circadian disruption in tumour microenvironments changes which immune cells show up, and when.

June 2026 · 4 min read

Epigenetics & AMR

Bacteria don't just evolve resistance. Sometimes they borrow it — without touching their DNA.

Epigenetic AMR is the version of antimicrobial resistance nobody is talking about loudly enough. Tolerance acquired without mutation, heritable within a generation, reversible in theory. The implications for how we design host-directed therapies are significant — and largely unexplored.

May 2026

Aging Biology

The immune system ages faster than most organs. And when it does, so does everything else.

Immunosenescence isn't simply about fewer T cells. It's about the quality of immune memory — whether the system can still distinguish self from non-self reliably. The oldest confirmed human had an anomalous immune profile at 115. We've never been able to replicate what she had.

April 2026

Dark Proteome

30% of human proteins have no known structure. That's not a gap in biology — it's a gap in our tools.

We call them intrinsically disordered proteins. They are overrepresented in cancer, neurodegeneration, and immune regulation. AlphaFold largely sidesteps them. The darkness is ours, not theirs — and it's where some of the most interesting biology is hiding.

March 2026

Neuroplasticity

The brain loses synapses during sleep. On purpose. Forgetting is a feature, not a failure.

Synaptic pruning during sleep is how the brain consolidates what matters and discards what doesn't. Microglia — the brain's resident immune cells — are involved. Which means the story of memory is also, in part, an immunology story. The two fields are converging in places nobody expected.
