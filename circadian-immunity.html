Your immune system runs on a clock. Tumours have figured out how to hack it.

Photo by Pawel Czerwinski on Unsplash

June 2026 · 3 min read

Waking during the day, sleeping at night — that's the routine most of us follow, except for the chronic all-nighters and shift workers who run their biology against the grain. We have internal clocks that govern metabolism, cognition, and immune function on a 24-hour loop. It's why your brain misfires on three hours of sleep — and as it turns out, your immune system is just as capable of losing its timing, though the mechanism runs far deeper than misplaced toothpaste.

The immune army — NK cells, T cells, B cells, neutrophils — circulates through the body on its own daily rhythm. Studies show their numbers and activity fluctuate by time of day, though most of this work is done in mice, whose active windows and nocturnal biology differ sharply from ours. I suspect this varies further across species — shaped by diet, metabolism, and whatever survival pressures shaped each species' clock in the first place.

Under normal circumstances, the immune system runs on schedule — until cancer cells stage what amounts to a slow, internal coup. Tumours show aberrant expression of core clock genes — CLOCK, BMAL1, PER, CRY — and use that disruption to build an immunosuppressive tumour microenvironment. They manipulate neighbouring immune cells by releasing metabolites and expanding immunosuppressive populations, effectively rewriting the body's own clock to work against it. Researchers are tracing this mechanism across NSCLC, breast, ovarian, prostate, and melanoma — and the manipulation appears to be type-specific, not universal.

It's strange to sit with — how something as simple as a 24-hour fluctuation carries this much hidden machinery for rewiring a body's defences. I'll let you sit with that too.
