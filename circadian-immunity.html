Your immune system runs on a clock. Tumours have figured out how to hack it.

June 2026 · 4 min read

There is a study that should have changed how we think about cancer surgery. Patients who underwent cardiac valve replacement in the afternoon had significantly better outcomes than those who had the same procedure in the morning. Same surgeon, same hospital, same operation — different time of day. The difference was measurable and it persisted at follow-up years later.

The study was published in 2018. It didn't make the rounds the way it should have. We still schedule surgeries based on operating room availability, not on the biology of when intervention might work best.

The reason outcomes differed isn't mysterious once you know where to look. The heart has circadian clocks embedded in its cells. So does the immune system. The two don't operate independently — and the time of day determines, in part, how each responds to stress, injury, and repair. What looks like a logistical detail turns out to be a biological variable we've been ignoring.

The immune system doesn't just respond to what it encounters. It responds according to when it encounters it.

Almost every immune cell type — T cells, NK cells, macrophages, neutrophils — shows circadian oscillation in number, function, and tissue trafficking. NK cell cytotoxicity peaks in the early morning. T cell migration to lymph nodes is gated by time of day. Cytokine production follows rhythmic patterns that have been conserved across evolution, suggesting they aren't incidental. The immune system, it turns out, has always known what time it is.

Which raises an uncomfortable question about tumours.

The tumour microenvironment is not a passive landscape. It is an actively maintained suppressive state — one that co-opts immune cells, rewires their function, and keeps effector populations exhausted or excluded. If immune cell activity is rhythmic, and tumours are sophisticated enough to suppress immune function, then the possibility follows naturally: tumours may be exploiting circadian timing to their advantage. Growing when immune surveillance is low. Suppressing when effector activity is high. Timing their own biology against the clock of the host.

The evidence for this is early but real. Circadian clock gene disruption — knocking out core clock components like BMAL1 or CLOCK — consistently accelerates tumour growth in mouse models. Shift workers, whose circadian systems are chronically disrupted, show elevated cancer risk across multiple tumour types. The WHO classified night shift work as a probable carcinogen in 2007, a designation that has only become more defensible since.

We built cancer immunotherapy around the assumption that the immune system is always ready to fight. It isn't. It depends on what time you're asking.

This has direct implications for how we design and administer immunotherapy. Checkpoint inhibitors work by removing the brakes on T cell activity — but if T cell activity is rhythmically regulated, the same dose given at different times of day may produce meaningfully different responses. The field has begun to take this seriously. There are now clinical trials explicitly designed around chronotherapy — timing drug administration to align with circadian windows of immune activity. They are small. They are early. But the results are consistently interesting.

What I keep returning to is simpler than clinical trial design. It is the strangeness of the basic fact — that the immune system has always operated in time, not just in space. We mapped its anatomy, its cell types, its signalling networks. We built drugs that target specific nodes in that network. And for decades we largely assumed the system was running continuously, like a machine left on. It isn't. It breathes. It has a rhythm. And the tumours growing inside us may have known that long before we did.

I don't have a clean conclusion for this. The field doesn't either. But I think the question of when — when to treat, when immunity peaks, when tumours are most exposed — is going to matter as much as the question of what in the next decade of cancer research. We've spent a long time asking what to target. It might be time to ask when.
